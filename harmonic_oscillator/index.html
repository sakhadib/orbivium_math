Using the Harmonic Oscillator Visualizer

This tool simulates a damped harmonic oscillator. Use the sliders to modify the parameters, and observe how they affect the motion and sound.

Mass Slider (\(m\))

Controls the mass of the object attached to the spring. Increasing the mass reduces the frequency of oscillation, as heavier systems oscillate slower.

Equation:

\[ f = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \]

Example: If the spring constant \(k = 50 \, \text{N/m}\) and the mass \(m = 5 \, \text{kg}\):

\[ f = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{50}{5}} \approx 1.59 \, \text{Hz} \]

What to Expect: When mass increases to \(10 \, \text{kg}\), the frequency decreases to approximately 1.12 Hz. The oscillator moves slower, and the sound pitch lowers.

Spring Constant Slider (\(k\))

Controls the stiffness of the spring. A stiffer spring oscillates faster due to stronger restoring forces.

Equation:

\[ f = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \]

Example: With \(m = 5 \, \text{kg}\), increasing \(k\) from 50 to 100 N/m raises the frequency from 1.59 Hz to 2.25 Hz.

What to Expect: Increasing \(k\) makes oscillations faster, and the sound pitch increases.

Damping Slider (\(b\))

Damping controls how quickly the oscillations fade. Higher damping reduces motion amplitude over time.

Equation:

\[ x(t) = A e^{-\frac{b}{2m}t} \cos(\omega t) \]

What to Expect: At \(b = 0.1\), oscillations fade slowly. At \(b = 1.0\), oscillations stop almost immediately.

Frequency Slider (\(f\))

Directly sets the oscillation frequency. Higher values produce faster motion.

What to Expect: Increasing \(f\) results in faster oscillations and a higher sound pitch.

Amplitude Slider (\(A\))

Amplitude controls the maximum displacement. Increasing amplitude makes the motion more pronounced.

Equation:

\[ x(t) = A \cos(\omega t) \]

What to Expect: Increasing \(A\) makes the motion visually larger, but the frequency and pitch remain unchanged.
